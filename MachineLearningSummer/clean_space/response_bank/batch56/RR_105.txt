To apply the Identification and Defective Argument Analysis (IDAA) to the statement "Canada is the best country to live in because it's better than the other countries!", we'll identify and explain the presence of defective argument types within the statement. Let's break it down:

1. **Generalization (G):** The statement assumes a universal characteristic ("best country") based on a comparison with "other countries" without providing concrete evidence or detailed comparisons. This is a generalization because it lacks specificity and thorough analysis of all relevant factors or countries.

2. **Inconclusive Reason (IR):** The reasoning provided is not sufficiently robust to conclusively establish the claim. Saying "because it's better" is a vague and subjective criterion that does not offer a comprehensive analysis or specific metrics for comparison.

3. **Faulty Repetition of Proposition (RR):** The argument is circular, essentially restating the proposition ("Canada is the best country to live in") without adding new information or supporting evidence. It repeats the claim in slightly different words ("because it's better than the other countries") without substantiating it.

4. **Presumptuous Consensus (FU):** Implicitly, the statement assumes a belief that is expected to be unchallenged or generally accepted ("best country to live in"), without acknowledging that this is a subjective opinion and may not reflect a consensus.

5. **Ambiguous Language (DEP):** Terms used like "best" and "better" are not defined clearly. What constitutes "best" or "better" can vary significantly depending on personal values, metrics of evaluation, and individual perspectives.

Summary of defective argument types present:
- **Generalization (G)**
- **Inconclusive Reason (IR)**
- **Faulty Repetition of Proposition (RR)**
- **Presumptuous Consensus (FU)**
- **Ambiguous Language (DEP)**
